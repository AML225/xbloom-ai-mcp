# XBloom Brewing Science Reference

## 1. Major Pour-Over Methods & Their Pour Structures

### 1.1 Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 Method (2016 World Brewers Cup)

**Core concept**: Divide total water into 40% (flavor control) and 60% (strength control).

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:15 |
| Grind | Medium-coarse (coarser than typical V60) |
| Temperature | 93°C (199°F) |
| Total brew time | ~3:25 |
| Number of pours | 5 (standard); 3-7 depending on strength preference |
| Wait between pours | ~45 seconds (wait until bed surface runs dry) |

**Standard recipe (20g dose, 300ml water):**

| Pour | Volume | Cumulative | Phase | Timing |
|------|--------|------------|-------|--------|
| 1 | 60ml | 60ml | Flavor (40%) | 0:00-0:15 |
| 2 | 60ml | 120ml | Flavor (40%) | 0:45-1:00 |
| 3 | 60ml | 180ml | Strength (60%) | 1:30-1:45 |
| 4 | 60ml | 240ml | Strength (60%) | 2:10-2:25 |
| 5 | 60ml | 300ml | Strength (60%) | 2:50-3:05 |

**Flavor adjustment rules (first 40% = 120ml):**

| First pour | Second pour | Result |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| 80ml | 40ml | More acidic, brighter |
| 60ml | 60ml | Balanced |
| 40ml | 80ml | Sweeter, less acidic |

**Strength adjustment rules (last 60% = 180ml):**

| Pours | Distribution | Result |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 pour | 180ml | Lightest body, cleanest |
| 2 pours | 90ml + 90ml | Medium strength, balanced |
| 3 pours | 60ml + 60ml + 60ml | Fullest body, strongest |

**Key technique**: Only begin next pour after the bed surface runs dry (~30 seconds after finishing each pour). No swirling or stirring. Spiral pour from center outward.

### 1.2 James Hoffmann Ultimate V60 Technique

**Core concept**: Two main pours after bloom, with strategic agitation (swirl + stir) for even extraction.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:16.7 (60g/liter) |
| Grind | Medium-fine |
| Temperature | 100°C (boiling) for light roasts; 15-20s off boil for dark |
| Total brew time | ~3:30 |
| Number of pours | 3 (bloom + 2 main pours) |

**Standard recipe (30g dose, 500ml water):**

| Step | Time | Action | Water | Cumulative |
|------|------|--------|-------|------------|
| Bloom | 0:00-0:10 | Spiral pour from center outward | 60ml (2x dose) | 60ml |
| Swirl | 0:10-0:20 | Circular motion to even the slurry | — | 60ml |
| Wait | 0:20-0:45 | Allow bloom/degassing | — | 60ml |
| Pour 1 | 0:45-1:15 | Aggressive center pour to churn bed, then concentric circles | 240ml | 300ml (60%) |
| Pour 2 | 1:15-1:45 | Gentle center pour to maintain level | 200ml | 500ml (100%) |
| Stir | 1:45-1:50 | Gentle back-and-forth stir (NOT whirlpool) | — | — |
| Wait | 1:50-2:05 | Let settle briefly | — | — |
| Swirl | 2:05-2:10 | 2-3 gentle swirls (Rao spin) | — | — |
| Drain | 2:10-3:30 | Complete drawdown | — | — |

**Key technique**: 60% of water should be added by the end of Pour 1. The stir at the end dislodges grounds from walls. The swirl levels the bed for even drawdown. Hoffmann explicitly recommends boiling water for most coffees.

### 1.3 Scott Rao V60 Method

**Core concept**: Minimal pours with emphasis on even extraction through the "Rao Spin" and wall-clearing stir.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:17 |
| Grind | Medium-fine |
| Temperature | ~95-100°C |
| Total brew time | ~2:15-4:30 (varies by dose) |
| Number of pours | 2-3 (bloom + 1-2 main pours) |

**Standard recipe (22g dose, ~340ml water):**

| Step | Time | Action | Water | Cumulative |
|------|------|--------|-------|------------|
| Bloom | 0:00 | Pour ~60ml, stir with spoon to wet all grounds | 60ml | 60ml |
| Wait | 0:00-0:45 | Allow bloom/degassing | — | 60ml |
| Pour 1 | 0:45 | Pour to ~210ml in concentric circles | 150ml | 210ml |
| Wait | — | Let water recede halfway down | — | — |
| Pour 2 | ~1:30 | Pour remaining ~130ml | 130ml | 340ml |
| Stir | ~1:45 | Gently stir to clear grounds from walls | — | — |
| Rao Spin | ~1:50 | Swirl dripper to create even, flat bed | — | — |
| Drain | — | Complete drawdown | — | — |

**Key technique**: The "Rao Spin" — swirl the filter cone to make the slurry spin, creating a flat bed. This reduces channeling and increases extraction consistency. Rao recommends using the plastic V60 for heat retention.

### 1.4 Melodrip / Controlled Agitation Brewing

**Core concept**: Disperse water through the Melodrip tool to reduce agitation, increasing clarity and sweetness. Pour amounts are expressed as multipliers of dose weight.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:16 |
| Grind | Medium (standard); finer for faster brews |
| Temperature | 93-96°C typical |
| Total brew time | ~4:30 (including 1-min bloom) |
| Number of pours | 4-6+ (many small controlled pours) |

**Standard recipe code: BK4sMD444 (16g dose, 256ml water):**

| Step | Action | Water |
|------|--------|-------|
| Bloom | Bare kettle pour, 4x dose | 64ml |
| Stir | Probe/stir for 5 seconds | — |
| Wait | 45s-1min | — |
| Pour 2 | Melodrip 4x dose | 64ml |
| Pour 3 | Melodrip 4x dose | 64ml |
| Pour 4 | Melodrip 4x dose | 64ml |

**Pour timing rule**: NOT time-based. Each pour begins when the slurry surface drains to ~1cm above the coffee bed. This adapts to the actual drain rate of each brew.

**Pour size rule**: Keep all pours between 3x-4x dose weight. Exceeding 4x increases water bypass (water flowing through filter without percolating through grounds).

**Ultra-clean variant (BK3sMD33331):**
- Bloom 3x, then all pours 3x, final 1x
- Slower, cleaner, but may lack body
- Total brew time longer due to lower water pressure from smaller pours

### 1.5 Lance Hedrick Techniques

#### 1.5.1 The 1-2-1 Method (Daily Brew)

**Core concept**: 1 pour, 2-minute bloom, 1 pour. Simplicity and repeatability.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:17 |
| Grind | Medium-fine |
| Temperature | 100°C (boiling) |
| Total brew time | ~3:50 |
| Number of pours | 2 (bloom + single main pour) |

**Recipe (18g dose, 306ml water):**

| Step | Time | Action | Water | Cumulative |
|------|------|--------|-------|------------|
| Bloom | 0:00 | Pour 54ml (3x dose) from HEIGHT for turbulence | 54ml | 54ml |
| Swirl | 0:05 | Aggressive swirl to remove clumps | — | — |
| Wait | 0:05-2:00 | Extended 2-minute bloom for full CO2 release | — | — |
| Main pour | 2:00 | Pour remaining 252ml heavily from high position, then slow circular when near filter edge | 252ml | 306ml |
| Stir | ~2:50 | Gentle top-layer stir to push fines to sides | — | — |
| Swirl | ~2:55 | Gentle swirl to flatten bed | — | — |
| Drain | 2:55-3:50 | Complete drawdown | — | — |

**Key technique**: The 2-minute bloom is the differentiator. Full degassing before the main pour means more even extraction. Pouring from height at the "stream breakup point" creates turbulence without needing a fine grind.

#### 1.5.2 Lance Hedrick's General Approach

Hedrick addresses the fines problem by using a **coarser grind combined with aggressive turbulence** (pouring from height). This achieves high extraction without the clogging issues that come from fine grinds + agitation. Recommended ratio: 1:15 to 1:17.

### 1.6 Osmotic Flow Method (Traditional Japanese)

**Core concept**: Center-only pouring creates a dome of coffee grounds. CO2 pressure and concentration gradients drive extraction through diffusion, not agitation.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:12.5 to 1:15 |
| Grind | Coarse |
| Temperature | 85-90°C |
| Total brew time | 3:00-4:00+ (slow, deliberate) |
| Number of pours | Continuous micro-pours |

**Technique:**
1. Tip dripper to 90-degree angle between water stream and coffee surface
2. Pour into a single point in the center of the coffee bed
3. After bloom deflates, draw very small circles while still pouring center
4. Never have much water in the cone at once — no slurry forms
5. Water passes through the coffee bed via osmotic pressure and gravity
6. CO2 escaping upward creates convection; water fills void spaces below

**Key technique**: This is NOT a slurry method. You are passing water through a bed of grounds, not mixing. The "dome" of grounds acts as a semi-permeable membrane. Very low agitation, maximum clarity. Continuous concentration gradient drives extraction.

### 1.7 April Brewer / Patrick Rolf Method

**Core concept**: Coarse grind, no post-pour agitation. All agitation comes from pour turbulence only.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:15 to 1:17 |
| Grind | Coarse (French press-like) |
| Temperature | 92-99°C |
| Total brew time | 2:00-3:00 |
| Number of pours | 4-5 pulse pours |
| Target TDS | 1.25-1.35 |

**Standard recipe (13g dose, 200ml water):**

| Step | Time | Action | Water |
|------|------|--------|-------|
| Pour 1 | 0:00-0:10 | 50ml spiral pour from center outward | 50ml |
| Wait | 0:10-0:35 | Bloom (no tap, no swirl, no stir) | — |
| Pour 2 | 0:35 | 50ml spiral pour | 50ml |
| Pour 3 | ~1:05 | 50ml spiral pour | 50ml |
| Pour 4 | ~1:35 | 50ml spiral pour | 50ml |

**Key technique**: NEVER tap, swirl, or stir. All agitation comes solely from the kettle's pour turbulence. Coarse grind compensates with extended contact time from multiple pulse pours. Very clean, tea-like cups.

### 1.8 Hario Switch (Immersion-Percolation Hybrid)

**Core concept**: Toggle between immersion (switch closed) and percolation (switch open) for best of both worlds.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | 1:16 |
| Grind | Medium |
| Temperature | 90-95°C |
| Total brew time | ~3:30 |

**Hybrid recipe:**

| Step | Time | Switch | Action | Water |
|------|------|--------|--------|-------|
| Bloom | 0:00 | Closed | Pour 60-70ml, full immersion bloom | 60ml |
| Wait | 0:00-0:40 | Closed | Immersion phase — all water stays in contact | — |
| Pour 1 | 0:40 | Open | Pour to 160ml, spiral pattern | 100ml |
| Pour 2 | 1:30 | Open | Pour to 260ml, consistent pour | 100ml |
| Immersion | 2:10 | Closed | Close switch, optionally lower temp to 70-80°C | — |
| Drain | 2:30+ | Open | Open switch, full drawdown by 3:30 | — |

**Key technique**: The closed-switch bloom means ALL bloom water stays in contact with grounds (unlike regular V60 where bloom water drains). This increases bloom-phase extraction significantly.

### 1.9 SEY Coffee High-Extraction / Low-Agitation Method

**Core concept**: Extremely fine grind (near espresso) with minimal agitation. Achieves 24%+ extraction with clean mouthfeel.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Ratio | ~1:16 |
| Grind | Very fine (200-300 microns from lock, barely coarser than espresso) |
| Temperature | ~93-96°C |
| Total brew time | 6-9 minutes |
| Agitation | Minimal to none |
| Target extraction | 24%+ (vs typical 18-22%) |
| Target TDS | ~1.37% |

**Key technique**: The paradox — fine grind produces abundant fines, but minimal agitation prevents their migration, so the bed doesn't clog. Results in extremely long brew times (6-9 min) but stable flow rates. Produces higher extraction with less astringency than high-agitation methods.

**Water**: Distilled water re-mineralized post-brew. 30-40 GH, 15-20 KH, equal Ca2+ and Mg2+.

## 2. Bloom Science

### 2.1 Bloom Volume vs. Dose

| Bloom ratio | When to use |
|-------------|-------------|
| 2x dose | Standard for most methods (Hoffmann: 2x) |
| 3x dose | Very fresh beans, aggressive degassing needed (Kasuya: 3x, Hedrick: 3x) |
| 4x dose | Melodrip method, ensures full saturation without agitation |
| 1.5x dose | Stale beans with minimal CO2 remaining |

### 2.2 Roast Freshness & Bloom Behavior

| Bean age | Bloom behavior | Adjustment |
|----------|---------------|------------|
| 1-3 days post-roast | Explosive bloom, massive CO2. Difficult to extract evenly | Wait longer. Bloom 45-60s. Consider extended bloom (2 min like Hedrick) |
| 4-14 days (peak) | Strong, dome-shaped bloom. Ideal window | Standard 30-45s bloom |
| 14-28 days | Moderate bloom, still good | Standard bloom, maybe slightly less bloom water |
| 28+ days | Weak or no bloom | Reduce bloom water (1.5-2x), shorter bloom (20-30s), grind finer to compensate for easier extraction |

### 2.3 CO2 Degassing Science

- Roasting generates CO2 trapped within the bean's cellular structure
- ~40% of CO2 leaves within first 24 hours post-roast
- Most CO2 escapes within 10-14 days
- Slow seepage continues for weeks
- CO2 creates a gas barrier around grounds that repels water, preventing even extraction
- The bloom step allows CO2 to escape before main extraction begins
- Darker roasts degas faster (more porous cellular structure from longer/hotter roasting)
- Light roasts retain CO2 longer (denser, tighter cellular structure)
- Grinding accelerates degassing dramatically (broken cells release trapped gas)

### 2.4 Bloom Timing by Roast Level

| Roast level | Recommended bloom time | Why |
|-------------|----------------------|-----|
| Light | 45-60 seconds | Dense beans, more trapped CO2, slower degassing |
| Medium | 30-45 seconds | Standard degassing rate |
| Dark | 20-30 seconds | Porous beans, fast CO2 release, already mostly degassed |

### 2.5 Bloom Agitation

- **Swirl** (Hoffmann preference): More even than stirring, less fines disruption
- **Stir** (Rao preference): More thorough wetting, but can disturb fines
- **Neither** (April method): Relies on pour turbulence only
- **Aggressive swirl** (Hedrick 1-2-1): Removes clumps, ensures full saturation for long bloom
- Hoffmann's experiments found swirling during bloom produced better tasting results than stirring

## 3. Pour Structure Theory

### 3.1 Number of Pours — Effects

| Pour count | Body | Clarity | Sweetness | Acidity | Agitation level | Best for |
|------------|------|---------|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 (single pour) | Low-medium | High | Medium | Medium | Low | Clean, bright cups (Rao style) |
| 2 | Medium | High | Medium-high | Medium | Low-medium | Daily brewing (Hedrick 1-2-1) |
| 3 | Medium | Medium-high | High | Medium | Medium | Most versatile (Hoffmann) |
| 4-5 | Medium-high | Medium | High | Medium-low | Medium-high | Controlled flavor (Kasuya 4:6) |
| 6+ | High | Lower | Variable | Lower | High | Very full body (Melodrip, micro-pours) |

### 3.2 Water Distribution Strategies

**Front-loading (60%+ in first main pour):**
- Hoffmann method: 60% by end of Pour 1
- Higher initial extraction, then taper
- Produces more brightness and complexity
- Risk: can create channeling if bed disturbed too aggressively

**Even distribution:**
- Kasuya 4:6 (equal 60ml pours) and Carlos Medina (equal 50ml pours)
- Most consistent and repeatable
- Produces balanced cup
- Easier to replicate

**Back-loading (more water in later pours):**
- Less common in modern specialty coffee
- Can produce muddier cups if bed has collapsed
- Sometimes used for very light roasts that need extended contact time

### 3.3 How Pour Size Affects Extraction

- **Many small pours (3x-4x dose each)**: More agitation events, longer total contact time, higher extraction, more body. But risk fines migration and clogging.
- **Few large pours**: Less agitation, faster total brew, cleaner cups but potentially lower extraction.
- **Critical insight**: Each new pour creates agitation when water hits the bed. More pours = more agitation events, regardless of swirling/stirring.

### 3.4 Declining Temperature Across Pours

- Pour-over is inherently a heat-loss process
- The first pour hits at kettle temperature, but the slurry cools during each wait period
- Most extraction happens early when temperature is highest
- **Intentionally using cooler water for later pours**: Some brewers advocate this (especially for dark roasts) to prevent late-stage bitterness extraction
- **Research against this**: Vibrant Coffee Roasters' blind tests found that dropping to 190°F or 170°F for later pours made cups thinner and less developed — for light roasts, keeping temperature high throughout is recommended
- **For dark roasts**: Reducing temperature by 3-5°C for final pours can reduce astringency
- **Best practice**: Preheat dripper and server. Keep kettle close. Minimize wait times to maintain thermal mass.

## 4. Bean-Specific Brewing Adjustments

### 4.1 By Roast Level

| Variable | Light Roast | Medium Roast | Dark Roast |
|----------|-------------|--------------|------------|
| Temperature | 93-95°C (or boiling) | 90-93°C | 85-90°C |
| Grind (XBloom scale) | 55-70 (fine-medium) | 70-85 (medium) | 85-100 (medium-coarse) |
| Bloom time | 45-60s | 30-45s | 20-30s |
| Bloom water | 3x dose | 2-3x dose | 2x dose |
| Ratio | 1:16 to 1:17 | 1:15 to 1:16 | 1:14 to 1:16 |
| Agitation | More (harder to extract) | Moderate | Less (extracts easily) |
| Flow rate | 3.0 ml/s (slower) | 3.0-3.2 ml/s | 3.2-3.5 ml/s (faster) |
| Pour count | 3-4 (more contact time) | 2-3 | 2-3 (less contact time) |
| RPM | 60-75 (preserve aromatics) | 75-90 | 90-120 |

**Why light roasts need more heat**: Dense cellular structure resists water penetration. Higher temperature = more kinetic energy to break down cell walls and extract solubles. Light roasts also retain more CO2.

**Why dark roasts need less heat**: Porous, brittle structure. Solubles are already partially broken down by extended roasting. Over-extraction happens easily, producing char and bitterness.

### 4.2 By Process Method

| Variable | Washed | Natural | Honey |
|----------|--------|---------|-------|
| Temperature | 95-98°C (higher) | 93-96°C (lower) | 93-96°C (moderate) |
| Grind | Finer (higher uniformity) | Coarser (extracts faster, more fines) | Medium |
| Ratio | 1:16 to 1:17 (more water for clarity) | 1:15 to 1:16 (less water, already sweet) | 1:15 to 1:16 |
| Agitation | More agitation OK | Less agitation (brittle, produces fines) | Moderate |
| Brew time | Up to 4 min | Can be as fast as 2 min | 2:30-3:30 |
| Pour count | 3+ (more contact time for complex acidity) | 2-3 (fewer pours = less fines disruption) | 3 |
| Flavor profile | Bright, clean, articulate acidity | Fruity, berry, wine-like, full body | Body of natural + brightness of washed |

**Natural process adjustments**: These beans are less dense and extract more quickly. They produce more fines when ground. Using fewer pours and less agitation prevents fines from clogging the filter and creating astringency. Slightly coarser grind compensates for easy extractability.

**Washed process adjustments**: Clean, uniform cell structure allows for finer grinds and more aggressive extraction without muddiness.

### 4.3 By Bean Density / Altitude

| Altitude | Density | Grind | Temp | Extraction behavior |
|----------|---------|-------|------|---------------------|
| 800-1200m (low) | Low density | Coarser | Lower (88-92°C) | Extracts easily, risk of over-extraction |
| 1200-1600m (medium) | Medium | Medium | Medium (90-93°C) | Standard parameters |
| 1600-2000m+ (high) | High density | Finer | Higher (93-95°C) | Resists extraction, needs more energy |

- High-altitude beans (SHB/SHG) have tighter cellular structure, more sugars, more complexity
- Dense beans require more force to grind = more uniform particle sizes = cleaner extraction
- Low-density beans fracture more randomly = more fines = adjust coarser and use less agitation
- Specialty coffee is typically grown at 1200m+ minimum

### 4.4 By Bean Age / Freshness

| Age | Bloom behavior | Grind adjustment | Other adjustments |
|-----|---------------|------------------|-------------------|
| 1-3 days (very fresh) | Explosive, uncontrollable | Coarser (+5-10 on scale) | Extended bloom (60s+), less agitation, higher ratio |
| 4-7 days (fresh) | Strong dome | Slightly coarser (+3-5) | 45s bloom, standard recipe |
| 7-21 days (peak) | Good dome, controllable | No adjustment needed | Standard recipe, peak flavor window |
| 21-35 days (rested) | Moderate bloom | No adjustment or slightly finer | May need slightly finer grind |
| 35-60 days (aging) | Weak bloom | Finer (-3-5 on scale) | Shorter bloom (25-30s), may need hotter water |
| 60+ days (stale) | No bloom | Significantly finer (-5-10) | Shortest bloom (15-20s), highest temp, more agitation |

**Resting period varies by roast level:**
- Light roasts: optimal after 7-14 days rest
- Medium roasts: optimal after 5-10 days rest
- Dark roasts: ready after 2-5 days rest (more porous, faster degassing)

### 4.5 Single Origin vs. Blend

- **Single origins**: Can dial in precisely for the specific bean's characteristics. Push variables toward that origin's strengths.
- **Blends**: Use middle-ground parameters. Ratio 1:16, medium grind, 91-93°C. The roaster has (ideally) balanced the blend for standard brewing. Avoid extremes of any variable.

## 5. Water Chemistry

### 5.1 SCA Water Standards

| Parameter | Target | Acceptable Range |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| TDS | 150 ppm | 75-250 ppm |
| Calcium hardness | 68 ppm CaCO3 | 50-175 ppm |
| Total alkalinity | 40 ppm CaCO3 | 40-70 ppm |
| pH | 7.0 | 6.0-8.0 |
| Sodium | < 10 ppm | — |
| Chlorine | 0 ppm | — |

### 5.2 Mineral Effects on Extraction

| Mineral | Effect on extraction | Effect on flavor |
|---------|---------------------|------------------|
| Calcium (Ca2+) | Moderate extraction power | Creamy body, enhanced mouthfeel |
| Magnesium (Mg2+) | Strong extraction power | Fruity notes, brightness, perceived sweetness |
| Bicarbonate (HCO3-) | Buffers acidity | Too high = flat/muted; too low = sharp/sour |
| Sodium (Na+) | Minimal extraction effect | Can add perceived sweetness at low levels |

### 5.3 TDS Impact on Coffee

| Water TDS | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| < 50 ppm (very soft) | Under-extraction: thin, sharp, hollow flavors |
| 75-150 ppm (ideal) | Balanced extraction, clear flavors |
| 150-250 ppm (moderate) | Still acceptable, slightly heavier body |
| > 250 ppm (hard) | Over-extraction risk, muddy flavors, scale buildup |

### 5.4 Roast-Specific Water Considerations

- Lighter roasts benefit from slightly higher mineral content (enhances sweetness and body)
- Darker roasts benefit from softer water (retains clarity, prevents over-extraction)
- Competition water is often custom-mineralized (e.g., Carlos Medina's 65ppm with equal Ca/Mg)
- TDS alone is a blunt tool — mineral composition matters more than total ppm

## 6. Advanced Extraction Concepts

### 6.1 Bypass Brewing

**What**: Adding water to brewed coffee AFTER extraction (water never touches grounds).

**Why**: Achieves high extraction yield at manageable strength. You brew concentrated (high TDS) then dilute to target strength.

**How it affects the cup differently than just using more brew water:**
- Brewing with more water = more extraction of late-dissolving compounds (including bitter ones)
- Bypass = high extraction of desirable compounds, then dilute = same extraction %, lower TDS
- Result: More flavor clarity at a given strength level

**Guidelines:**
- Start with 10ml bypass water, taste, increase incrementally
- Mithilesh Vazalwar's rule: 2g bypass water per 1g coffee dose
- Most bypass brewers use 60-80% of total water for extraction, add 20-40% as bypass
- Target TDS for finished cup: 1.15-1.45% (SCA recommendation)
- Formula: Bypass water = (Brewed coffee ml x Actual TDS%) / Target TDS% - Brewed coffee ml

### 6.2 Percolation vs. Immersion Extraction

| Property | Percolation (pour-over) | Immersion (French press, cupping) |
|----------|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Mechanism | Fresh water continuously replaces extracted solution | Water saturates over time |
| Extraction efficiency | Higher (fresh water maintains concentration gradient) | Lower (slurry saturates, slowing extraction) |
| Cup flavor correlation | Strongly correlated with extraction SPEED of compounds | Strongly correlated with ABUNDANCE of compounds in bean |
| Acidity | Higher, brighter | Lower, more muted |
| Body | Lower, cleaner | Higher, heavier |
| Bitter compounds | Less (fast-extracting acids/sugars over-represented) | More (given enough time, bitter compounds catch up) |
| Typical grind | Finer (faster flow needs more surface area) | Coarser (long steep needs less surface area) |
| Typical extraction yield | 18-22% | 16-20% |

**Hybrid methods** (Hario Switch, AeroPress): Combine both. Immersion phase for body and balanced extraction, percolation phase for clarity and separation. Jonathan Gagne developed a unified equation for calculating extraction in mixed methods.

### 6.3 Fines Migration & Channeling

- **Fines**: Sub-100-micron particles produced during grinding
- **Migration**: During brewing, fines move downward with water flow and accumulate at the filter
- **Clogging**: Accumulated fines block filter pores, slowing drawdown dramatically
- **Channeling**: When parts of the bed clog, water finds paths of least resistance, creating channels where some grounds are over-extracted and others under-extracted
- **Agitation accelerates fines migration**: More stirring/swirling = fines move faster to filter
- **The paradox**: Agitation increases extraction evenness but also increases clogging risk
- **Solutions**:
  - Less agitation with finer grinds (SEY method)
  - More agitation with coarser grinds (Hedrick method)
  - Dispersion tools like Melodrip to reduce impact force
  - Coarser grinds can tolerate more stirring/swirling
  - Finer grinds are more sensitive to any agitation

### 6.4 Agitation vs. Grind Size: Different Extraction Levers

| Variable | What it changes | Flavor effect | Risk |
|----------|----------------|---------------|------|
| Finer grind | More surface area (permanent change to all pours) | More extraction across the board | Clogging, bitterness |
| More agitation | Refreshes concentration gradient at particle surface (temporary) | More even extraction, potentially more total extraction | Fines migration, channeling |
| Hotter water | Increases solubility and diffusion rate | Extracts everything faster | Over-extraction of bitter compounds |
| Longer brew time | More contact between water and grounds | More total extraction | Astringency from late-extracting compounds |

**Key insight**: Grind size and agitation produce DIFFERENT extraction profiles even at the same total extraction yield. Finer grind extracts more uniformly from each particle. More agitation extracts more from the bed as a whole but can create uneven particle-level extraction.

## 8. Grind Size Guide (XBloom 40-120 scale)

| Range | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| 40-55 | Very fine | Not typical for pour-over (espresso-like). SEY ultra-high extraction method only. |
| 55-70 | Fine-medium | Light roasts, dense beans, high extraction needed, washed process |
| 70-85 | Medium | Most versatile, balanced extraction, medium roasts |
| 85-100 | Medium-coarse | Dark roasts, naturally processed beans, Kasuya 4:6, osmotic flow |
| 100-120 | Coarse | April brewer style, cold brew, very dark roasts |

## 9. Temperature Guide

| Roast Level | Temperature Range | Why |
|-------------|-------------------|-----|
| Light | 93-95°C (some use boiling) | Dense beans need more heat energy to extract |
| Medium | 90-93°C | Balanced extraction |
| Dark | 85-90°C | Porous beans extract easily; lower temp prevents bitterness |

## 10. Pour Patterns

- **Centered**: Water hits center only. Gentlest. Good for bloom and osmotic flow. Least agitation.
- **Circular**: Water traces a circle. Even saturation, standard pour-over choice. Moderate agitation.
- **Spiral**: Water spirals outward from center. Most surface coverage, most agitation. Best for main pours.

## 11. Flow Rate (3.0-3.5 ml/s)

- **3.0**: Gentler, more contact time, better for fine grinds and light roasts
- **3.5**: Faster, less contact time, prevents over-extraction with coarser grinds

## 12. Grinder RPM (60-120)

- **60-75**: Less heat generation, preserves delicate aromatics. Best for light roasts.
- **75-90**: Good all-rounder.
- **90-120**: Faster grinding, fine for medium/dark roasts.

## 13. Extraction Science Quick Reference

### Under-extracted (sour, thin, salty)
Fix: Finer grind, hotter water, more agitation, longer bloom, slower flow

### Over-extracted (bitter, astringent, dry)
Fix: Coarser grind, cooler water, less agitation, shorter bloom, faster flow

### Well-extracted (sweet, balanced, clean finish, pleasant aftertaste)
Target: 18-22% extraction yield (specialty standard)
High-extraction methods can reach 24%+ with proper technique (SEY method)

### Extraction order of flavor compounds
1. **Acids** extract first (sour, bright)
2. **Sugars/sweetness** extract second (sweet, caramel, fruit)
3. **Bitter compounds** extract last (bitter, astringent, dry)

The art of brewing: extract through sweetness, stop before excessive bitterness.

### Key Variables (in order of impact)
1. **Grind size** — Biggest lever. Changes surface area dramatically.
2. **Water temperature** — Second biggest. Controls extraction rate.
3. **Pour structure** — How many pours, their relative sizes.
4. **Agitation** — Fine-tuning. Use sparingly.
5. **Flow rate** — Smallest adjustment (3.0-3.5 is a narrow range).

## 14. Water Volume Math

Total water = dose_g x ratio

Example: 15g dose x 16 ratio = 240ml total water

Pour volumes must sum to approximately this total. The XBloom machine will flag significant mismatches.

### Typical Distribution (3-pour, 240ml total)
- Bloom: 45ml (19%) — 3x dose
- Main: 110ml (46%)
- Final: 85ml (35%)
